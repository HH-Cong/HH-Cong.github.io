It is chanllenge to grow such SMBHs by the limited 'Eddington accretion rate', even at 100% duty cycle. Two alternatives are proposed to explain the formation and evolution of these SMBHs. The first scenario is "Heavy Seed BHs". In this scenario, heavy seed BHs can form by direct collapse of massive cloud under some certain conditions (so called DCBH). The other scenario is "Rapid growth". In this case, the seed BHs (possibly stellar mass BHs) can grow at Super-Eddington rate. I investegate the rapid growth of seed BHs via simulations.

    • This figure shows evolutionary tracks of heavy Seed BHs in BH mass-galaxy mass diagram. The heavy seed BHs (10^5 solar mass, seed at z=15), can always grow faster than host galaxies, reaching the overmassive region in the mass-mass diagram compared to local relations. In this model, we propose a subgrid feeding (and feedback) model for seed BHs at galatic centers. Such a subgrid model can facilitate the rapid growth of seed BHs at high-z universe, when the outflow strength is moderate (p ~ 0.5-0.7). For strong outflows (p>0.7), the rapid growth of light seed BHs are significantly suppressed.

We reproduced varied morphologies of cold gas similar to the observed ones in Perseus (i.e., radial filaments, horseshoe filament, blueloop like structures) in simulations. The origin of these cold filaments also indicating the energetics of the AGN activities. The subsequent evolution of cold gas may also indicate the positive AGN feedback in a 10-20 Myr time delay.

    Formation of SMBHs at high-z (z>6) universe

  • The rise of the question

    As the figure below summarizes that observation have already revealed the existence of SMBHs (marked by colored dots) at redshift beyond 6 when the universe was less than 1 Gyr old. It was too young for the universe to have such massive BHs. Because in the traditional fashion, it took the universe 45 Myr to e-fold the mass of a seed BH. For a 10 solar mass BH seeded at z=35, the growth tracks are indicated as the colored curves with different duty cycles (mared with the curves). It would be marginally possible to grow all observed BHs in such an idealized model. Therfore, it is natural to seek alternatives or more physically motivated models.

    The possible solutions

    The general solutions to explain the existence of SMBHs at high-z universe are: i) growing from a heavy seed BH, ii) growing at a super-Eddington rate. In the first scope of this project, I only focus on the "super-Eddington growth model".

    Heavy Seeds Model

    Generally, there are three scenarios to form heavy seed BHs. i) remnant of Pop III stars, ii) runaway collapse in dense stellar clusters, iii) DCBH.

    The Super-Eddington growth model

    Generally, the accretion onto the central objects are limited to Eddington accretion rate. However, for BHs, the radiation emited from accretion could be trapped and thus the accretion rate may exceed the Eddington limit, as long as dense gas is fed to cental BH.

    • The feedback from AGN activity in form of outflows

  • AGN feedback

    AGN feedback in form of outflows (jets, winds) and thermal energy is believed to essential in regulating the evolution of host galaxies and central SMBHs. In observations, local galaxy clusters, e.g., Perseus Cluster are perfect laboratories to investigate the effects of AGN feedback on host galaxies.

    The evolution of AGN outflows

    In Perseus cluster, their are vaired morphologies of cold gas filaments (ref) which can not be easily explained by cold gas entrainment and acceleration by stellar wind (stellar feedback) (ref). Following Qiu et al (2020), we perform 3D hydrodynamical simulations to investegate the cold gas formation and evolution as a result of cooling of AGN driven hot outflows.

    Purpose

    We explain the morphological and dynamical evolution in Qiu, Hu et al (2021). We discuss the supersonic signature and the origins of the cold gas filaments in Hu et al (2022). The studies on the formation and evolution of cold gas filaments in Persues cluster can provide us with the information of past AGN activities. The latest AGN activity could be at least 10 Myr ago, with kinetic energy around 1e57 ergs, and feedback power large than 2e44 erg/s.
